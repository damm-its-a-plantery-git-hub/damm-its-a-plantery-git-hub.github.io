<h1>Why I’m Done With Disposable Tech</h1> 

<p>We’ve entered an era where owning a device doesn't actually feel like ownership. Last month, my three-year-old laptop started dying within an hour of being unplugged. In the past, this was a $40 fix and ten minutes of work. Today, it was an exercise in pure frustration that convinced me our tech is being built to fail.</p>

<img style="width: 100%;" src="images/repair_tools_visual.jpg" alt="3D tools and electronic components">

<div>
    <h2><br><b>The Wall of Glue and Screws</b></h2>
    <p>Modern design has traded "fixability" for "thinness," and we are the ones paying the price. When I opened my device, I wasn't met with components I could swap, but with a fortress of anti-repair tactics: </p>
    <ul>
        <li><p><b>Proprietary Screws</b>: Unique heads that require specialized kits just to turn the first bolt.</p></li>
        <li><p><b>Industrial Adhesives</b>: Batteries glued so firmly to the chassis that removal risks a fire hazard.</p></li>
        <li><p><b>Soldered Memory</b>: If one tiny chip fails, the entire motherboard—the heart of the machine—is trash.</p></li>
        <li><p><b>Software Locks</b>: Parts that won't "talk" to the system unless a certified technician enters a secret code.</p></li>
    </ul>
</div>

<div id="imagedivider">   
    <img id="content_image" src="images/recycle_bin_3d.png" alt="3D illustration of electronic waste">
    <p>Every year, millions of tons of "unfixable" hardware end up in landfills simply because a single $5 component was made impossible to reach or replace.</p>
</div>

<div>
    <h2><br><b>The Cost of Convenience</b></h2>
    <p>We are told that these designs are necessary for water resistance or sleek profiles, but companies like Framework and Fairphone are proving that isn't true. You can have a beautiful, powerful machine that still uses standard screws. The reality is that "planned obsolescence" is a business model, not a design necessity. By making things impossible to fix, manufacturers ensure you'll be back in their store spending another thousand dollars in three years.</p>
</div>

<div>
    <h2><br><b>Conclusion</b></h2>
    <p>We’ve traded longevity for convenience, but at the cost of our wallets and the environment. Moving forward, my tech "vote" goes to the companies making hardware that’s built to be serviced, not just replaced. It’s time we stop treating thousand-dollar electronics like they’re single-use items. If you can't open it, you don't really own it.</p>
</div>
